  1. Introduction
  1. River dolphin conservation in the Ganga River in Sahibganj district, Jharkhand: towards a protected area

The Ganga River stretch that flows along the Sahibganj district, Jharkhand, forms an important corridor and reference site for monitoring freshwater biodiversity for two primary reasons. It lies in an area with significantly high density of Ganges River Dolphins, India’s National Aquatic Animal, and is contiguous with the Ganga in Bihar’s Bhagalpur and Katihar districts upstream and the Farakka barrage on the Ganga is located downstream. The region has a significant level of fishing and other human impacts related to waterway navigation and river infrastructure development, which are important threats to Ganges river dolphins. As a result, the stretch is of utmost importance in terms of establishing a long-term monitoring program. At present, the Jharkhand Forest Department has expressed interest in designating this area as a Conservation Reserve or wildlife sanctuary for Ganges River Dolphins. With this, there is a need to start planning for effective conservation and management, taking into account not only ecological status but also social issues related to human livelihoods.

  1. The Ganga River in Jharkhand: area description

The Ganga River stretch within the Jharkhand state boundary has a length of approximately 37 km by river (as of February 2024). From Manihari (Katihar, Bihar) to Farakka (Malda/Murshidabad, West Bengal), the river stretch of 98 km includes the Sahibganj district en route. Within Jharkhand, four relatively well-defined river segments of the main channel of the Ganga were surveyed in February 2024: 1) Lal Bathani to Sakrigali, 2) near Nurpur, 3) Rajmahal to Narayanpur Char, and 3) Pranpur (Jitnagar) to Begamganj-Dogachhi Char. The population status we report here is presented both at the scale of the overall 98 km stretch and the ~37 km channels around Sahibganj, Nurpur, Rajmahal, and Pranpur (Figure 1).

Ganges River Dolphin population estimation and apparent trends as of February 2024

We estimated Ganges River Dolphin population size using the independent double-observer methodology from a single motorized country boat with two observer platforms/teams in this stretch. The population estimate was determined at 252 (range or 95% C.I.: 230-274) for the 98 km stretch. Of this, the ~40 km stretch in Jharkhand recorded an estimate of 118 (range: 102-134), with 48 adults, 64 sub-adults, and 6 calves. Interestingly, while the ‘metapopulation’ showed a decline from February 2023 when we did the previous survey (Table 1), the population within Jharkhand showed a clear increase (Table 2). Our estimates point towards the channels in Jharkhand seeing an increasing trend of river dolphin population size. On the contrary, a clear decline is seen in the river stretch in West Bengal.

  1. Threats to Ganges river dolphins in the stretch

The primary known threats to Ganges river dolphins in the Ganga river stretch of Jharkhand are related to impacts of shipping (from vessel engine and cavitation noise, dredging, and associated pollution risk), and fisheries-induced mortality from accidental entanglement as well as occasional illegal hunting. Despite these threats, it is a positive sign that the Ganges river dolphin population shows a clear indication of stability or even increase in the channels within Jharkhand, despite the overall decline in the stretch. This could be interesting to probe more carefully in future surveys. In this area, river water availability per se is not a constraint, but river channel and flow alterations by port construction, bridge construction, and embankments are an emerging issue. The nature of threats calls for technological interventions as well as socio-economic engagement with local river-dependent communities. These approaches, being attempted by our team WCT, may be considered in the following recommendations section, as they may help mitigate some of these impacts and minimize the risks to aquatic wildlife to the extent possible. In the build up towards designating a protected area or reserve for Ganges river dolphins in the Jharkhand stretch, pre-planned interventions and careful social engagement will be critical.

Recommendations & Future Work Proposed

  1. To minimize Ganges river dolphin bycatch in fishing nets, it is crucial to adopt technological innovations that can reduce spatial overlap between fishing activity and dolphins. We have developed a GPS-enabled and geofencing-based buzzer device that can be installed on boats to tag them, in order to help fishers to proactively move away from areas marked virtually as hotspots or high-risk aggregation sites. Incentives need to be designed for fishers to voluntarily take part in such safety-oriented use of the river space. Tagging of boats with such devices may especially help government authorities in efficient fisheries management upon the event of designation of the protected area for river dolphins on the Ganga in Jharkhand. We provided an initial introduction to the device in July 2023 and would like to soon have a field demonstration of the same in the summer months of 2024.
  2. The Ganga river stretch in the area is part of National Waterway No. 1 and an integral part of the infrastructure of this waterway is the Sahibganj terminal and ferry traffic between Maniharighat in Bihar, Sahibganj, Sakrigali, and Rajmahal. According to the mitigation measures to reduce ecological risk and impact proposed by the Environmental Impact Assessments by the Inland Waterways Authority of India itself, there are some important directions: 1) propeller casing to reduce underwater noise and risk of strike injury to river dolphins, 2) use of bubble curtains to reduce the impact of dredging sediment disturbance and noise at the river bottom, and 3) testing the effectiveness of speed limits proposed as a mitigation measure to reduce propeller hits and minimize river dolphin deaths or injuries. However, despite these apparently useful measures, there has been no field testing or application so far. We can help monitor the impacts of the first two mitigation measures using underwater acoustic recordings and analyses, with systems that we are currently developing. For speed limits, the buzzer device described above can be used to tag ferries, larger vessels, or ships, so that they can detect hotspots and exercise the required caution.
  3. With the designation of a new protected area in the planning horizon, it is crucial to involve river-dependent fishing communities and other local settlements in the planning process right from Day Zero. Their support needs to be ensured and rewarded with adequate incentives as well as participatory processes that lead to democratic decision-making and settlement of rights in terms of using the river ‘commons’ as a multi-use space without compromising on conservation. This will help the decision-making authorities not make the same mistakes as have been made in cases such as the Vikramshila Gangetic Dolphin Sanctuary (in Bhagalpur, Bihar, upstream of this area) – where fishing rights were never settled through formal processes. This led to conflicts with fishing groups over access, rights, and use of the river for basic livelihoods versus conservation threats from fishing. These conflicts are serious and unfortunate, as they limit the potential for sustained conservation outcomes. Averting such problems will need thoughtful integration of river wildlife conservation and capture fisheries management, by developing locally agreed rules to minimize fishing threats to endangered species. Our team will be keen to help the forest and fisheries departments in the processes of community participation, incentive provision, and allocation/settlement of rights in the area in case the protected area declaration moves forward.
